## 1. Sponsorship tiers & pricing

Current as of Season 2 (Mar–Jun 2026). Two standard offerings. Season Partner is **not** being offered this season — flag any interest above the Episode Partner tier to Briana for custom scoping.

### Tier 1 — Episode Partner · $1,500

The flagship package. Tied to a single episode and artisan.

**Inclusions:**
- Host-read mid-roll ad (60–90 seconds) written collaboratively with the brand
- "Brought to you by [Brand]" credit in the episode intro
- Logo in episode credits
- Dedicated links in episode description on Spotify and YouTube
- Dedicated newsletter ad block in the *Trade Secrets* newsletter for that episode
- Dedicated social media sponsor spotlight post on launch day (IG + LinkedIn)
- **Per-episode category exclusivity** (no competing category in the same episode)

**Best fit for:**
- Brands with clear alignment to a specific trade or artisan in the season
- Local SF businesses looking to reach design-conscious consumers
- Tools and platforms artisans genuinely use
- Heritage, lifestyle, or craft-adjacent brands testing podcast sponsorship

**Tier sales goal for Season 2:** 2 Episode Partnerships sold = $3,000 revenue.

### Tier 2 — Community Supporter · $500

A lower-commitment entry point. Great for small businesses that want visibility without the full ad integration.

**Inclusions:**
- Social media post on episode launch day (IG + LinkedIn)
- Newsletter mention in the episode-launch Substack
- Logo on the Trades Show partners page

**Best fit for:**
- Local SF small businesses with aligned values but smaller budgets
- First-time sponsors testing the audience before committing to the Episode Partner tier
- Brands that want to signal support for the artisan movement without needing an ad read

### Above Episode Partner — flag to Briana

Anything larger (season-level integration, co-branded content, custom activations) is **not in the standard offering for Season 2**. The agent does not quote or negotiate these. Route to Briana for custom scoping.

**Signals a custom conversation might fit:**
- National-scale sponsor with a clear alignment to the full season arc
- Sponsor asks about season-level exclusivity
- Sponsor asks about co-produced content, branded segments, or events
- Sponsor's budget is clearly well above $1,500 and they want more surface area

### Pricing posture

- **Never lead with pricing in a cold email.** Pricing lives in the one-pager / deck. The first email sells the story and the alignment.
- **If asked directly in a reply:** be fully transparent about both tiers.
- **Don't discount on first ask.** If a sponsor pushes back on $1,500, route them to the Community Supporter tier at $500 rather than negotiating the Episode Partner price down. Preserving tier integrity matters for future seasons.
- **Multi-episode bundles:** If a sponsor wants two episodes, offer $2,700 (10% off $3,000 list) as a standing discount. Three or more → flag to Briana.

### Why $1,500 (rationale for the agent's context)

At the previous $1,000 price point, the Episode Partner tier was underpriced against comparable niche/craft podcasts with similar audience quality. Host-read ads on values-aligned shows with 2K engaged listeners typically command $1,500–$2,500. $1,500 is the considered rack rate — not aggressive, not a discount.

## 2. What's NOT for sale

Hard rules. These are non-negotiable regardless of sponsor size or offer.

- **Pre-roll ad spots.** The Trades Show format puts story first; pre-rolls break the opening.
- **Editorial control.** Sponsors do not influence which artisans are featured, which questions are asked, or how the episode is cut.
- **Negative mentions of competitors.** The host-read ad celebrates the sponsor; it does not compare them.
- **Data on individual listeners.** Only aggregate metrics are shared.
- **The Corral jobs board placements.** The Corral is a separate venture with its own sponsorship structure.
- **Guest bookings in exchange for sponsorship.** Guest decisions are editorial, not commercial.
- **Endorsement of products Briana hasn't used or can't authentically speak to.**
- **Political or religious content.**
- **Season-level integration** (not offered Season 2 — see above).
- **Brand-side removal of negative commentary** or steering conversations away from a competitor.

## 3. Ideal sponsor profile & fit test

### The fit test (run this first)

Before any research or draft effort, score the sponsor against these 5 points:

1. **Audience overlap.** Their customers are design-literate consumers 30–45 who value craft, quality, and intentional purchases.
2. **Values alignment.** Their brand ethos sits inside the Analog Renaissance — heritage, handmade, local, slow, craft, sustainability.
3. **Reachability.** A $1,500 ask is approachable for their size — not too small to afford, not too big to bother.
4. **Timeliness.** They're actively growing, hiring, launching, or marketing. Not in retreat, not acquired, not between CMOs.
5. **Integrity.** Their product or service is something Briana would genuinely use, recommend, or respect.

**Scoring:**
- **5/5:** Priority — research further, draft immediately.
- **4/5:** Strong — research further, queue.
- **3/5:** Research further, then decide.
- **2/5 or below:** Skip. Do not pitch at a lower tier.

### Categories that FIT

#### Tier A — Highest fit (lead with these)

Named brands + category examples. This is where the agent should source leads first.

- **SF-based specialty retailers** with maker/craft-adjacent customer base
- **Premium consumer brands built on craft or materials** — Filson, Pendleton, Taylor Stitch, Buck Mason, Hedley & Bennett
- **Tools & equipment for makers** — Benchmade, Leatherman, Lodge Cast Iron, KitchenAid, Craftsman, Milwaukee Tool
- **Specialty food & beverage with artisanal production** — Fly By Jing, Graza, Fishwife, Omsom, Ghia, De Soi, craft coffee roasters, natural wine shops, small-batch distillers, bakeries, chocolatiers
- **Heritage apparel & accessories** — made-in-USA brands, traditional footwear, leather goods, workwear with a craft story
- **Independent bookstores, print shops, stationery makers**
- **Home & lifestyle DTC** — Parachute, Brooklinen, Material Kitchen, Made In, Great Jones, East Fork, Farmhouse Pottery
- **Wellness & sensory goods aligned with slow-living** — Vellabox, Boy Smells, P.F. Candle Co., Osea

#### Tier B — Strong fit

- **Platforms artisans use to run their businesses** — booking, scheduling, portfolio, invoicing, small business tools (especially if UX is considered and brand has taste)
- **Financial services for small business / creative entrepreneurs** — Ramp, Mercury, Found, QuickBooks Self-Employed, Square, Shopify
- **Insurance & benefits for self-employed artisans** — Stride, Catch, Collective, HoneyBook
- **Creative tools & platforms** — Squarespace, Format, Faire (wholesale), Substack
- **Workwear & durable apparel** — Carhartt, Dickies (premium lines), Flint and Tinder
- **Continuing education / skill platforms** — MasterClass, Skillshare, Domestika
- **Design-forward home goods** — ceramics, linens, furniture, lighting from small and mid-size makers
- **Sustainability and slow-fashion brands** with real supply chain stories
- **Trade schools, apprenticeship programs, craft education** (often grant-funded, but worth the relationship)
- **Cultural institutions** — museums, craft fairs, design weeks, maker festivals
- **Slow travel & hospitality** — Airbnb (craft-stay hosts), Hipcamp, Autocamp, boutique hotels

#### Tier C — Stretch fit (require specific angle or warm intro)

- **Shopify, Stripe** — platforms artisans use, but large enough that a $1,500 pitch needs a creative hook or warm intro
- **Heath Ceramics, Shinola** — lifestyle brands with built-in craft narratives; compelling fit but their sponsorship programs are usually structured and competitive
- **Premium publication partnerships** — Kinfolk, Cereal, Cabana, The Gentlewoman, Sight Unseen (potential co-branded content partners, not traditional sponsors)
- **Heritage luxury houses with real craft lineage** — Hermès, Red Wing (approach only if warm intro exists)

### Categories that DON'T fit (hard no)

Skip these entirely. Do not pitch at a lower tier.

- **Direct-to-consumer mass brands** with no craft story (fast fashion, dropshipping, generic e-commerce)
- **Crypto, Web3, NFT, gambling, sports betting, alcohol delivery apps**
- **Extractive industries** — oil, mining, fast food chains, industrial agriculture
- **AI products that replace creative or craft labor** — this is the sharpest no. The show's thesis is human skill's resilience in the AI age; sponsors selling AI-generated art, AI-written copy, or AI-replaced creative work are an ideological conflict, not a fit.
- **Pure ad-tech, martech, or B2B SaaS** with no consumer story
- **Mega-corporate buyouts of heritage brands** where the original ethos has been degraded
- **MLMs, coaching-certification pyramids, "entrepreneurship" info products**
- **Political organizations or PACs**
- **Mass-market undercutters of small makers** — Temu, Shein, Wish
- **Brands currently in public controversy, labor dispute, or legal issue**
- **Competitors of existing partners or current Aura/Fractal clients** — always check the conflict list before reaching out

### Tech/AI special case

Tech and AI brands can fit, but only if the positioning is authentically craft-supportive (e.g., tools for artisans running businesses). The audience is skeptical of AI bluster; the show's frame is "artisanship during the AI revolution." A wrong-footed tech sponsor alienates listeners.

- **Right-footed:** Notion, Square, Shopify, Squarespace (tools artisans actually use)
- **Wrong-footed:** AI art generators, AI-written content platforms, anything that replaces the kind of work the show celebrates

### Geographic priorities

1. **San Francisco first.** Season 2 is SF-anchored. ~19.6% of audience is in SF. Local sponsors get local audience.
2. **New York second.** ~11.3% of audience. Many lifestyle/heritage brands that fit are NYC-based.
3. **National third.** Only when fit is exceptional and brand has a relevant national story.
4. **International — skip by default.** Logistics, currency, and time zones don't work for a $1,500 deal.

### Size heuristic (is $1,500 in their budget?)

- **<10 employees:** usually yes, but marketing budget may not exist. Route to founder directly.
- **10–100 employees:** sweet spot. Marketing lead exists, budget exists, $1,500 is approachable.
- **100–500 employees:** still good. Look for Brand Marketing, Partnerships, or Community Manager roles.
- **500+ employees:** possible but harder. Usually requires warm intro or highly specific activation angle.

### Historical context

- **First sponsorship push:** Season 2 (Mar 2026). No partners closed yet as of this file's creation.
- **Season 2 revenue target:** $3,000 total = 2 Episode Partnerships.
- **Sell philosophy:** Every first-season sponsor is a long-term relationship bet. Close two great ones rather than ten cold ones.

## 4. Integration guidelines

How sponsors actually appear in The Trades Show content. These guidelines protect the audience experience, which is what makes sponsorship valuable.

### The guiding principle

> Sponsors get associated with craft, not clutter.

Every integration choice flows from this. If an integration would make an episode feel more like an ad and less like a documentary, it doesn't happen.

### Host-read mid-roll ad (Episode Partner tier)

**Format:**
- **60–90 seconds**, recorded by Briana in her voice
- Placed at natural mid-episode break, never interrupting a key story moment
- Introduced with "Brought to you by [Brand]" — not "This episode is sponsored by..."
- Closes with a specific call-to-action (URL, code, or action)

**What we include:**
- The sponsor's actual story — one or two sentences about why they exist
- A specific product, feature, or value proposition
- A personal angle from Briana when honest — "I use this in my own studio" or "I found them through [specific context]"
- A clear next step for the listener

**What we don't include:**
- Generic marketing copy lifted from the sponsor's homepage
- Superlatives Briana wouldn't naturally say ("revolutionary," "game-changing," "#1")
- Comparisons to competitors
- Urgency-driven language ("limited time!", "act now!")
- Anything Briana hasn't personally verified or experienced

### Collaborative ad writing process

Ads are **host-read, always** and **written collaboratively.** Brands submit key talking points, required disclosures, and brand voice guidance. Briana adapts into her own voice. Final script approved by the brand before record.

**One mid-roll per episode, max.** The show is not ad-stacked. Listeners notice, and it degrades the product for everyone.

**Contextual placement.** Whenever possible, the ad lives near a natural moment in the conversation (e.g., a tool brand placed near a discussion of craft equipment). This is a feature, not a burden.

**No competing sponsors in the same episode.** If the episode partner is a knife brand, no other cutlery-adjacent sponsor runs. This is the per-episode category exclusivity in action.

### Category exclusivity

**Episode Partners get per-episode category exclusivity.** Category is defined mutually in the agreement (e.g., "direct-to-consumer kitchen tools" — not the broader "kitchen goods"). This means:
- No other brand in that defined category runs in the same episode
- Other sponsors (different categories) can still run in the same episode
- Season-level exclusivity is not offered at this tier

### Approval windows

- **Ad copy finalized:** 5 business days before episode launch
- **Sponsor review:** one round of revisions on the ad script
- **Host-read recorded:** 7 days before drop
- **Sponsor sees written mentions** (newsletter, social, description) 5 days before drop — for factual corrections only, not rewrites
- **All sponsor assets due:** 10 days before the episode drops (logo, brand description, talking points, CTA)

### Off-limits integrations

- **No endorsements of products Briana hasn't used or can't authentically speak to**
- **No political or religious content**
- **No integrations that would compromise an artisan guest's own business** (e.g., a mass-market competitor to the featured bladesmith)
- **No pre-roll programmatic ads** — sponsor model is host-read only

### "Brought to you by" intro credit

**Format:**
- Spoken in the first 30 seconds of the episode
- One sentence maximum
- Example: "This episode is brought to you by [Brand] — [one clause positioning them]."

**What it's not:**
- A second ad read
- An opportunity to list product features
- A place to insert a promo code

### Newsletter integration (*Trade Secrets*)

**Format:**
- Dedicated sponsor block in the episode's newsletter send
- Brand logo, 2–3 sentence brand description, one link with UTM
- Placed below episode summary, above closing section

**What we don't do:**
- Dedicated sponsor-only sends
- Sponsor content disguised as editorial
- Multiple sponsor blocks in one send
- Promotional subject lines mentioning the sponsor

### Social media sponsor spotlight

**Format:**
- One dedicated post (carousel or single image) on launch day
- Tagged in Trades Show IG and LinkedIn; cross-posted by Briana
- Visual style matches Trades Show brand (handpainted type, B&W photography where possible)
- Copy frames the sponsor inside the episode's story, not as a standalone ad

**What we don't do:**
- Story-only mentions without a dedicated feed post
- Engagement-bait captions ("Tag a friend who loves craft!")
- Competitions or giveaways requiring audience action (unless custom-scoped and approved)
- Sponsor takeovers of Trades Show accounts

### Episode credits & description

**In episode credits:**
- Sponsor logo displayed on screen during end credits
- Name spoken as part of the credit roll

**In YouTube/Spotify description:**
- Sponsor link with UTM, below episode summary, above timestamps
- Brief sponsor tagline — one sentence

### Integration matching — pairing sponsors to episodes

When proposing an Episode Partnership, the agent should suggest an episode match based on thematic or product alignment.

| Sponsor type | Episode match |
|---|---|
| Kitchen tool / cookware brand | Stuart Brioza (chef) or Sophie Smith (baker) |
| Knife or blade brand | Elias Sideris (bladesmith) |
| Glassware, home goods with ceramics/glass | Momoko Schafer (glassblower) |
| Outdoor, surf, California lifestyle | Danny Hess (surfboard maker) |
| Heritage workwear, denim, boots | Any episode, strong across all |

The match isn't mandatory — a sponsor can select any episode — but the agent should always suggest the most natural fit in the initial pitch.

### Production requirements from the sponsor

Once a deal closes, the sponsor provides:
- Logo (vector preferred: SVG or EPS)
- One-paragraph brand description (for newsletter)
- Approved talking points for the host read (2–3 bullet points, not a full script)
- A call-to-action (URL, code, or specific product page)
- A single approver for final copy

## 5. Outreach cadence

The rhythm of sponsor outreach. How often, how many touches, what channel, when to stop.

### Channel priority

1. **Email — primary.** Every first touch goes through email. Email is where decisions get made and where one-pager / deck shares live.
2. **LinkedIn — secondary / warm-up.** Connection request + short note before the email, when a name is visible and mutual. Not a replacement for email.
3. **Instagram DM — rarely.** Only for small, founder-led, IG-native businesses where email is hard to find. Never for brands with >20 employees.
4. **Phone, text, cold call — never.** Out of scope.

### First-touch CTA strategy

**Default:** "I'd love to send over our one-pager."

A one-pager is lower friction than a full deck, respects the reader's time, and is easier to forward internally.

**Exceptions (when to escalate or combine CTAs):**

| Context | CTA |
|---|---|
| Default cold outreach | One-pager |
| Warm intro (referral) | One-pager + optional "hop on a quick 15 if it resonates" |
| Enterprise / complex brand where one-pager is insufficient | Both: one-pager + 20-minute call offer |
| Sponsor replies expressing interest | Send deck + offer 15-min call with 2–3 calendar options |

**Never:**
- "Let me know if this is of interest" — too passive
- "Let's jump on a call" as cold CTA — too heavy
- Any CTA that requires commitment before they've seen anything

### Touchpoint sequence — 3 touches maximum

A full sponsor sequence is **3 touches before the agent marks them as "no response — closed"** and moves on. No sponsor is worth more than three touches from a cold start.

#### Touch 1 — Initial pitch
- **When:** Tue–Thu preferred, 9am–11am PT
- **Channel:** Email
- **CTA:** One-pager (or escalated variant per table above)

#### Touch 2 — Warm follow-up
- **When:** 5–7 business days after Touch 1, only if no response
- **Channel:** Email, reply on same thread
- **Content preference:** Episode-tied — reference a specific Season 2 episode currently in production and why the sponsor fits it (see voice.md Exemplar E). Falls back to generic warm bump (Exemplar F) if no episode tie available.
- **CTA:** Same as first, OR softer: "Happy to drop this thread if it's not a priority"

#### Touch 3 — Final check-in
- **When:** 14 business days after Touch 2, only if no response
- **Channel:** Email, reply on same thread
- **Content:** Short, no new ask. Signals the sequence is ending.
- **CTA:** None — or at most "Circle back for Season 3?"

**After Touch 3 with no response:** mark closed. Do not re-pitch this season. Revisit next season if alignment is still strong.

### Post-interest cadence (warm sequence)

Once a sponsor replies expressing interest, a different sequence kicks in.

#### Step A — Deck send + call offer
- Send deck within 24 hours of their reply
- In same email, offer 15-minute call to walk through it
- Include 2–3 calendar link options

#### Step B — Deck follow-up
- **When:** 4 business days after sending deck, if no response
- **Content:** "Did the deck land okay? Happy to answer questions by email if a call is too much."
- **CTA:** Reply on any question; book the call if they want

#### Step C — Soft close
- **When:** 7 business days after Step B, if no response
- **Content:** Short, specific: "Are you thinking about this for Season 2 or should I reach out for Season 3?"
- **CTA:** Binary — gives them a graceful exit or a reason to come back

#### Step D — Warm revisit
- **When:** No close after 30 days
- Add to warm-lead list
- Re-engage at a natural moment (new episode drop, season launch, press feature)
- Do NOT send another cold pitch

### Frequency rules for the agent

- **Batch size per run:** 5 leads in TEST mode, 20 leads in FULL mode
- **Sends per week, max:** 10 fresh outreach emails. More and Briana can't keep up with replies; voice quality slips.
- **Follow-ups per day, max:** No more than 5 follow-up touches queued in any one day. Spread them out.
- **Time of day:** Tue–Thu, 9am–11am PT. Not Mondays (inbox chaos), not Fridays (weekend dropoff), not evenings.

### Cadence exceptions

**Event-driven acceleration:** If the sponsor has a time-sensitive moment (product launch, store opening, campaign), a faster cadence is okay — but still max 3 touches, compressed into 10 business days instead of 21.

**Seasonal windows:**
- **Q1 (Jan–Mar):** Annual sponsorship planning window. Many brands set marketing budgets in January.
- **Q2 (Apr–Jun):** Season 2 is live. Best window for mid-season sponsor pitches tied to specific episodes.
- **Q3 (Jul–Sep):** Summer lull. Response rates drop. Reduce volume; use time to build next season's pipeline.
- **Q4 (Oct–Dec):** Planning window for next season. Pitch "reserve your spot for Season 3" rather than current-season.

**Conference / event tie-in:** If Briana is speaking at or attending an event the sponsor is also at (HumanX, SF Design Week, Bay Area Craft Collective), the agent can propose an in-person meeting as the CTA on Touch 1 instead of a one-pager. Rare — flag these for Briana to approve first.

### Logging requirements

Every touch gets logged to the Notion Relational Management DB:
- Contact name + org
- Touch number (1, 2, 3)
- Channel
- Date sent
- Status (Draft / Sent / Replied / Closed / Warm)
- Next action + date, if any
- Full draft saved to "Draft Message" field

**No touch is "done" until the log is updated.** Agent never sends without logging.

## 6. Personalization minimums

The non-negotiables. Every pitch from the Sponsorship Director includes **all** of the following. If any one is missing, the draft is not ready.

### The five minimums

#### 1. A specific, verified observation about the sponsor
Not "I love what you're building." Not "Your brand is amazing." Something concrete: a product, a store visit, a recent announcement, an Instagram post, a press feature, a value they lead with, the way they describe their process. One sentence, but real.

> ❌ "I've been following [Brand] for a while and love your work."
> ✅ "I picked up a [specific product] from your Hayes Valley shop last month, and the way you described the [specific material or technique] on the tag stuck with me."

**Source check:** observation must come from the sponsor's actual website, a recent social post, a press feature, or a verified first-hand experience. If the agent cannot find a real specific, it flags the lead for manual research rather than inventing one.

#### 2. A named, correct person
Every pitch goes to a specific named human. No "Hi team," no "To whom it may concern," no generic info@ addresses. If the agent cannot find a specific named contact, the lead gets flagged, not pitched.

**Preferred titles, in order:**
1. Founder / CEO (for brands under 50 employees)
2. Head of Marketing / Marketing Director
3. Partnerships Lead / Brand Partnerships Manager
4. Community Manager / Community Lead
5. Brand Manager

**Name verification:** first name spelled correctly, pronouns respected if findable, last name and title confirmed against a current source (LinkedIn within 90 days, their website, recent press). Titles change — verify, don't assume.

#### 3. A specific alignment statement
Why *this* brand, why *The Trades Show*, why *now*? One sentence connecting the sponsor's ethos or audience to the show's.

> ❌ "I think we'd be a great fit."
> ✅ "Your audience of design-minded home cooks overlaps directly with the people who find us through episodes like our Sophie Smith feature at Butter & Crumble."

#### 4. A specific integration suggestion
Pair the sponsor with a specific Season 2 episode or artisan in the initial pitch. Shows thought, not blast.

See the pairing matrix in § Integration Guidelines. The agent proposes one pairing in the initial email. Sponsor can choose differently in reply.

#### 5. A clear, soft CTA
"I'd love to send over our one-pager" — with a specific offer to tailor it to their brand. Never a vague "let me know." Never a heavy ask (a call, a meeting) except per the CTA escalation rules.

### Beyond the five — every pitch must also have

- **Subject line** that references the sponsor (not generic) — see voice.md subject line patterns
- **Briana's name and role** in signature, first email intro, or both
- **One link max** in body — to thetradesshowpod.com or a specific episode, never the one-pager/deck (that comes after)
- **Length: 110–160 words** for cold outreach body. Under 100 is too thin; over 200 is too much.
- **Mobile readability** — short paragraphs, no more than 3 lines each

### Tiering personalization by target value

- **Community Supporter outreach ($500):** minimums 1–4 required; named episode optional
- **Episode Partner outreach ($1,500):** all 5 required
- **Above Episode Partner (flagged to Briana):** all 5 required, plus custom 1-page proposal with specific integration mocked up, plus audio sample

### The sanity check before sending

```
[ ] Does the first paragraph include a specific, verified observation?
[ ] Is the recipient a named human with a correct title?
[ ] Is there a clear "why this brand, why now" alignment statement?
[ ] Is there a specific episode or artisan integration suggested?
[ ] Is the CTA the right one for the context (one-pager default, call per exceptions)?
[ ] Is the subject line specific to them, not generic?
[ ] Is the email 110–160 words?
[ ] Would Briana send this email as written, or would she edit it first?
```

If any box is unchecked, draft is not ready. Revise.

### What "personalized" does NOT mean

- **Not "stuffed with their brand name five times."** Once is enough; twice is natural; three is pushy.
- **Not longer.** A 300-word email with sponsor details reads as more generic than a 120-word email with one perfect observation.
- **Not flattery.** "I adore your brand" is not personalization, it's noise.
- **Not mentioning their competitor.** Even favorably. Keep comparisons out of the pitch entirely.

## 7. Never-do list

Hard rules. These apply to anyone — Briana, a VA, or this AI agent — doing outreach on behalf of The Trades Show.

### Never misrepresent the show

- **Never inflate audience numbers, download counts, or demographic stats.** If you say 2K engaged, it's 2K — not "over 2,000 and growing fast." Brands verify; the ones worth partnering with walk the moment they catch it.
- **Never claim guests, partnerships, or press that haven't been confirmed in writing.**
- **Never promise deliverables that aren't in the package** (e.g., "we could probably do two episodes for that price") without Briana's sign-off.

### Never misrepresent the relationship

- **Never pretend to have met someone you haven't,** been referred by someone who didn't refer you, or had a prior conversation that didn't happen.
- **Never use "per our last conversation"** as a cold open. It's a tell that torches trust.
- **Never fabricate mutual connections** or LinkedIn activity.
- **Never fake an "I was just listening to your podcast" moment** that didn't happen.

### Never be manipulative

- **No fake scarcity** ("only 2 slots left" when there are 8)
- **No fake deadlines** ("this rate expires Friday" when it doesn't)
- **No guilt-based framing** ("we're a small team trying to make it — please help")
- **No "just checking in if you saw my last email?"** as the entire email. Add value or don't send.
- **No A/B testing emotional manipulation tactics** on real prospects.

### Never compromise the editorial product

- **Never promise a brand that Briana will endorse a product** she hasn't used
- **Never promise editorial influence** (e.g., "we can mention your brand in the interview itself" — interviews are not for sale)
- **Never promise guest bookings in exchange for sponsorship**
- **Never offer to remove negative commentary** or steer conversations away from a competitor

### Never handle money or contracts without Briana's sign-off

- **Never quote custom pricing without approval**
- **Never sign, countersign, or commit to terms** in email
- **Never accept payment to non-Trades-Show accounts**
- **Never offer refunds, make-goods, or discounts** on your own authority

### Never use AI to fake being human

- **If an agent (human or AI) is doing outreach, disclose it or don't pretend otherwise.**
- **Never scrape and send from purchased lists.**

### Never damage the brand

- **No outreach to competitors** of confirmed sponsors without disclosure
- **No outreach to brands in active public controversy** (labor, legal, safety)
- **No outreach to brands whose values conflict with the show's** (fast fashion, exploitative labor, AI-replacing-craft)
- **No mass "breakup emails"** ("I'll assume you're not interested and won't follow up") — they read as petulant and follow you

### Never skip the logging

- **Every touch logs to the Notion Relational Management DB** before it sends
- **Every draft logs to `agent_outputs`** via `logOutput()`
- **Every status change updates both** — Notion and `agent_outputs`

Text-form distillation of the partnership deck (`The_Trades_Show_Partnerships_Deck.pdf`) so the agent can reference contents without parsing the PDF each run. **Update this file whenever the deck is updated.**

### Key narrative beats the agent can reference

- **The Analog Renaissance** — the cultural thesis. Backlash to AI-driven everything; consumer appetite for handmade.
- **The Cobbler Origin Story** — In 2023, Briana brought a pair of beat-up secondhand leather loafers to a local cobbler; they came back pristine. Revealed craft as artistry, sparked The Trades Show. **Use sparingly in cold emails** — it's memorable, but it's also in the Behind the Show slide, so don't front-load it in cold emails where the deck will follow. **Never use "Gucci loafers"** — the brand name contradicts the craft-over-luxury ethos.
- **"San Francisco, the global epicenter of AI"** — Season 2 framing. Artisans whose livelihoods predate the internet, practicing blocks from AI company offices.
- **"Artisanship" vs "craftsmanship"** — Briana reclaims the word. "Ship" = movement + tech launch. The Trades Show is the storytelling branch; The Corral is the infrastructure branch.

### What the deck does NOT say — don't invent

- **Specific sponsor case studies** (no closed sponsors yet)
- **Guaranteed performance metrics**
- **CPM, CPA, or any performance-adjacent math**
- **Revenue projections**
- **Download-count floors or ceilings**

### When to send the deck vs. one-pager

- **Cold outreach → one-pager** (default)
- **Sponsor reply expressing interest → deck + call offer**
- **Enterprise / complex sponsor → both one-pager and deck available depending on interest level**

### When to update this deck summary

- Whenever the deck is rebuilt (new version, new season)
- When audience stats change materially (female % shifts >3 points, audience crosses round threshold)
- When a tier is added or pricing changes
- When new press, awards, or closed sponsors become part of the pitch
