## 3. Story angle inventory

**Note:** The cobbler origin story has been **retired as a standalone angle** (formerly Angle 4). It is retained as the founder-profile hook, and may emerge naturally within pitches — but never as the cold-pitch opener. See § 8 Cobbler Story Rationing.

### Angle 1 — Going Analog in the AI Capital of the World
SF is the epicenter of AI, but blocks away, artisans practice trades that predate the internet. The Trades Show explores the "both-and" of craft and technology — not anti-tech, but pro-human.

- *Best for:* Tech/culture outlets (Wired, The Verge, Vox), SF local press
- *Strength:* Timely, contrarian, easy to visualize
- *Caveat:* This is Season 2's SF-specific framing. Don't force on non-SF pitches.

### Angle 2 — What AI Can't Learn: The Intelligence in Your Hands
The sensorial body of knowledge that defines artisan trades doesn't live in an LLM. The Trades Show goes inside workshops to reveal intelligence that exists beyond the dataset.

- *Best for:* Long-form culture (The Atlantic, The New Yorker), AI/tech outlets, Kevin Delaney @ SF Standard
- *Strength:* Intellectually rich, philosophical, differentiating

### Angle 3 — The Oldest Trades in the Newest City
Before code, there was craft. The Trades Show tells SF's story through the people who've been shaping it by hand for decades.

- *Best for:* SF local press (Chronicle, KQED), history/culture outlets
- *Strength:* City-pride angle, generational depth, visual storytelling

### Angle 4 — [RETIRED — Cobbler Origin Story]
Previously a standalone angle. Retired because it's the origin, not a pitch lead. The cobbler story now lives in Briana's founder profile and may surface when a journalist specifically asks "how did you start this?" See § 8 Cobbler Story Rationing.

### Angle 5 — The Disappearing Trades (Data-Driven)
97% of US cobbler shops are extinct. 58% of heritage crafts are endangered. 2.1M skilled trades workers needed by 2030. The Trades Show is building a living archive.

- *Best for:* News outlets, data-friendly publications (Vox, Bloomberg), policy/workforce coverage
- *Strength:* Urgency, hard numbers, trend story

### Angle 6 — Gen Z Wants to Work with Their Hands
60% of Gen Z workers interested in skilled trades for job security. $345B handcraft market growing 8% annually. Cultural shift underway.

- *Best for:* Career/workforce outlets, trend pieces, generational coverage
- *Strength:* Counternarrative to "everyone wants to code," backed by data

### Angle 7 — The Artisanship Ecosystem (Bigger Picture)
The Trades Show is the storytelling arm of a larger ecosystem: The Corral (national artisan jobs board), The Artisan Mag, and a mission to build infrastructure for the artisan economy.

- *Best for:* Entrepreneurship/startup outlets, design/innovation press
- *Strength:* Shows scale of vision beyond a podcast
- *Caveat:* Only use when the outlet cares about the business/ecosystem angle. Most press pitches should lead with the show.

### Angle 8 — AI in Service of the Trades
**Refined from original "Solo Founder Building with AI" framing.** The angle isn't "solo founder using AI to grow" — it's "the thoughtful tension between building infrastructure *for* an industry that AI can't replicate, *using* AI to uplift it." The mission is the hero; AI is the tool in service of trades that automation can't touch.

- *Best for:* Tech/AI outlets where the intersection of craft and technology is genuinely the story (SF Standard under Kevin Delaney, The Verge future-of-work coverage, Wired, Fast Company)
- *Strength:* Differentiating — most AI coverage is about replacement. This is about collaboration and protection.
- *Caveat:* Never pitch as a pure tech-founder story. The artisan mission must stay foregrounded. Test: if the headline would be "Founder Uses AI to Build Company," it's wrong. If it would be "Inside the Use of AI to Protect What AI Can't Make," it's right.

### Angle 9 — Individual Artisan Profiles (Episode-Specific)
Each episode is a standalone pitch. A chef, a bladesmith, a glassblower, a surfboard shaper, a baker — each has their own story, audience, and press angle.

- *Best for:* Trade-specific publications, food press, local press
- *Strength:* Highly targeted, easy for journalists to say yes to a single profile

## 4. Personalization minimums

Non-negotiable. Every pitch clears all six before queueing.

### The six minimums

#### 1. First name, correctly spelled
No "Hi there," no "Hi team," no "To whom it may concern." If the first name can't be verified, the pitch doesn't send — flag for manual research.

#### 2. Correct role and outlet, verified within 90 days
Titles change. The Kevin Delaney lesson: he became SF Standard Editor-in-Chief in June 2025. A pitch addressed to "Julie Makinen" would be disqualifying and insulting.

Verification sources: LinkedIn (within 90 days), the outlet's staff page, a recent byline.

#### 3. One specific, true observation about the journalist or outlet
Either (a) a specific piece they wrote and what struck you, OR (b) a specific beat / section they cover that connects to the pitch.

> ❌ "I love your work."
> ✅ "Your piece on [title or topic] from [recent month] — the thread about [specific detail] stayed with me."

Source check: must come from the outlet's actual archives. If the agent cannot find a real, specific reference, it falls back to show-first mode rather than inventing one.

#### 4. A real reason this outlet fits this show
Not "you cover craft" (generic) but "your SF-first lens combined with your future-of-work coverage is exactly the framing for this" (specific to that outlet).

#### 5. A named episode or moment where the show would belong
Don't pitch "a partnership" or "coverage" generically. Pitch the Stuart Brioza episode (with air date), the Elias Sideris profile, the season arc. Specificity converts.

#### 6. A concrete, low-pressure ask
"Would you be open to coffee?" or "Any fit for [specific section]?" or "Open to a 20-minute conversation?" — never "let me know if you're interested."

### Beyond the six — every pitch must also have

- **Subject line** that references the journalist, outlet, or a specific moment
- **Length appropriate to type** (cold pitch: 150–250; follow-up: 60–120; check-in: 40–80)
- **One link max in body** (press deck attached, not linked in body)
- **Sign-off** as "Warmly + with gratitude, Briana" or "Warmly, Briana"
- **No inflated metrics** (2K is 2K; 32.5K views is 32.5K — no puffing)

### Do not send if any of these are true

- The journalist was pulled from a list and the agent hasn't checked their byline in the last 30 days
- The agent doesn't know who covers the beat currently
- The same pitch would work for three other journalists without editing
- The personalization section could be pasted into any other outlet's brand without changes
- The outlet is on the off-limits list (see § 5)
- The journalist is on the journalist-conflicts list (currently empty; grows as needed)

### The sanity check before sending

```
[ ] Does the first paragraph include a specific, verified reference?
[ ] Is the recipient a named human with a correct, current title?
[ ] Is there a clear "why this outlet, why now" alignment?
[ ] Is a specific episode, artisan, or season moment suggested?
[ ] Is the ask low-pressure and specific?
[ ] Is the subject line specific, not generic?
[ ] Is the pitch length appropriate?
[ ] Is the angle matched correctly to the outlet type?
[ ] Would Briana send this email as written, or would she edit it?
```

If any box unchecked, revise before queueing.

## 5. Off-limits angles & outlets

### Off-limits angles — confirmed

- **"Anti-tech" framing.** The Trades Show is not against AI or technology. The narrative is "both-and" — using advanced tools to recover what is essentially human. Any pitch positioning the show as Luddite or anti-progress misses the point.

- **Poverty / struggle porn.** Artisans are not charity cases. The show celebrates mastery, not hardship. Don't lead with "dying trades" without also leading with "thriving artisans."

- **Briana as a "content creator."** She's a founder, host, and filmmaker — not an influencer. Framing matters.

- **Artisanship LLC details prematurely.** The entity doesn't exist yet publicly. Don't pitch the ecosystem as a formal company until legal structures are in place. Lead with The Trades Show; mention The Corral as context, not co-pitch.

- **Overselling metrics.** 2K+ engaged and 32.5K YouTube views are real and respectable for an independent docuseries — but don't frame them as massive scale. The story is quality: 19.6% SF-local concentration and 5★ Spotify rating tell the right story.

### What is NOT off-limits — important clarification

The tech/AI framing is **Season 2's specific hook**, not the show's essence. The Trades Show is fundamentally a show about modern artisans and their craft — technology is backdrop for this season, not thesis. This matters because:

- **Craft, design, and heritage outlets are in-scope targets** — even though they don't want the AI tension. The show works as a pure-craft pitch for these outlets. See Tier 4 outlet list and Exemplar D.
- **It's acceptable to pitch the show without mentioning AI at all** when pitching craft/design/trade publications.
- AI tension is primarily a pitch tool for SF local outlets (where it's a city-identity story) and tech/AI outlets (where it's the journalist's beat).

### Outlets to avoid — confirmed

- **Outlets that would reduce the show to "podcast about crafts"** — if an outlet's coverage is purely listing-based with no narrative depth, the pitch isn't worth sending. The story deserves the writer, not just the slot.
- **Pay-to-play outlets** — no paid features, sponsored content disguised as editorial, "as seen in" schemes.
- **Outlets misaligned with the show's values** — anything that would undermine the artisan-first, slow-business, craft-as-thriving-practice ethos.

- Previously dismissive in coverage or conversation

## 6. Outreach cadence

Matches Sponsorship Director's cadence for system-wide consistency.

### Channel priority

1. **Email — primary.** Every first touch via email. Decisions happen in email.
2. **LinkedIn — secondary / warm-up.** Connection request + short note can precede email when appropriate. Not a replacement for email outreach.
3. **Instagram DM — rarely.** Only for small-outlet journalists where email is hard to find and IG is their active platform. Never for major outlets.
4. **Phone, text, cold call — never.** Out of scope.

### First-touch CTA

**Defaults for press:**
- Founder-first mode (local / warm): "Would you be open to coffee?" or "Open to a conversation?"
- Show-first mode (national / trade): "Any fit for [specific section]?" or "Open to a 20-minute conversation about this?"
- Podcast guest: "Would you be open to a conversation about a guest slot?"
- Speaking: "Happy to send more context or jump on a call"
- Awards: ask = application submission; follow-up language only if there's a natural moment

### Touchpoint sequence — 3 touches max

A full sequence is **3 touches before the journalist is marked "no response — closed"** for the season. No journalist is worth more than 3 touches from cold.

#### Touch 1 — Initial pitch
- **When:** Tue–Thu preferred, 9am–11am PT
- **Channel:** Email
- **Voice mode:** Context-dependent per voice.md

#### Touch 2 — Warm follow-up
- **When:** 7–10 business days after Touch 1, only if no response
- **Channel:** Email, reply on same thread
- **Content:** Add genuine new context — a new episode dropped, a press mention, a relevant cultural moment (HumanX-type tie-in). Never generic "just checking in."
- **CTA:** Same ask, softer; offer them a graceful exit

#### Touch 3 — Final check-in
- **When:** 14 business days after Touch 2, only if no response
- **Channel:** Email, reply on same thread
- **Content:** Short, no new ask. Signals end of sequence.
- **CTA:** "Last note from me — circle back at a natural moment"

**After Touch 3:** Mark closed. Do not re-pitch this season. Revisit next season or when a new angle genuinely fits.

### Post-response cadence (warm sequence)

Once a journalist responds positively:

- **Reply within 24 hours** of their response
- **If they ask for more:** send whatever was requested (trailer, deck, clips, specific artisan bio)
- **If they propose a meeting:** confirm, send calendar options, follow up with post-meeting thank-you (Exemplar J)
- **If they indicate interest but no specific next step:** propose the lightest next step that matches their energy — "happy to send the trailer" or "happy to jump on a quick 15 when works"
- **Handoff to Briana** for any decision requiring judgment about scope, format, exclusivity, or embargo

### Frequency rules for the agent

- **Batch size per run:** 5 leads in TEST mode, 20 in FULL mode
- **Sends per week, max:** 10 fresh outreach emails. More and Briana can't keep up with replies; voice quality slips.
- **Follow-ups per day, max:** 5 queued touches. Spread them out.
- **Time of day:** Tue–Thu, 9am–11am PT. Not Mondays (inbox chaos), not Fridays (weekend dropoff), not evenings.

### Cadence exceptions

**Event-driven acceleration:** If tied to a specific time-sensitive moment (conference, episode air date, breaking craft-adjacent news), a faster cadence is okay — but still max 3 touches, compressed into ~10 business days.

**Seasonal / show-cadence windows:**
- **Pre-season (Mar 2026):** Trailer + Season 2 announcement — strong moment for broad outreach
- **Mid-season (Apr–May 2026):** Episode-specific pitches at their air dates
- **End-of-season (Jun 2026):** Season wrap-up + early Season 3 planning angles
- **Between seasons (Jul–Aug 2026):** Reduced volume; relationship-maintenance only

### Logging requirements

Every touch logs to Notion Outreach DB:
- Contact name + outlet
- Touch number (1, 2, 3)
- Channel
- Date sent
- Status (Draft / Sent / Replied / Meeting / Closed / Warm)
- Next action + date
- Full draft saved to "Draft Message" field

No touch is "done" until logged.

## 7. Relationship rules for journalists

1. **Always personalize.** Every pitch opens with a real reference to the journalist's work.
2. **Never mass-blast.** No BCC, no template spray. Each email to one person.
3. **Follow up once, then twice maximum.** Touch 1 → Touch 2 (7–10 days) → optional Touch 3 (+14 business days). After that, silence is the answer.
4. **Reference their past work specifically.** Not just their beat — a specific piece. Proves you read them.
5. **Lead with relationship, not coverage.** The SF Standard and Gazetteer pitches both led with personal connection and asked for a conversation, not a feature. Model for founder-first mode.
6. **Be honest about the ask.** "I'd love to build a press rapport" is more disarming than pretending you just want to chat.
7. **Match the ask to their format.** A feature for the Chronicle, a mention for a newsletter, a guest spot for a podcast, a partnership for an aligned publication. Don't ask everyone for the same thing.
8. **Don't pitch the same outlet twice without reason.** Check the Outreach DB before reaching out. If someone at an outlet was already contacted, either wait or find a different angle / journalist at that outlet.
9. **Use real-world moments as openers only when real.** Conferences, neighborhood events, mutual connections — un-fakeable and powerful when genuine. Never invent one.
10. **Attach the press deck, don't over-explain.** The deck does the heavy lifting on stats, artisans, visuals. The email is the human connection; the deck is the proof.
11. **Honor warm threads.** Matt Haber @ The Gazetteer is a warm contact. Do not send fresh outreach to Gazetteer contacts — that's Briana's thread to manage.
12. **Credit past coverage.** When citing Flourish Careers feature, link it. Journalists notice credibility signals.

## 8. Cobbler story rationing

The cobbler origin story is Briana's most emotionally resonant hook. Because it's been in the press deck and is being used in founder-profile pitches, it's at risk of overuse — which would dilute its impact.

### Rules

1. **Treat as 1 source.** The cobbler story appears once per outlet, once per moment. Not twice in the same pitch. Not in a pitch and then again in a follow-up.
2. **Never lead cold pitches with it.** It's the origin, not the hook. Pitches open with the current moment — what the show is doing now, who's in Season 2.
3. **Retire "Gucci loafers" framing permanently.** The brand name contradicted the craft-over-consumer-luxury ethos. Use the visceral repair moment, not the brand signal.
   - **PR Director + Talent Scout use:** "a pair of beat-up secondhand leather loafers" (or "a pair of beat-up leather loafers" when shorter is better) — the visceral repair moment is the story
   - **Funding Scout uses:** "secondhand loafers" — matches Briana's actual phrasing in submitted grant applications
   - Both are correct for their context; the difference is deliberate
4. **Let it emerge naturally.** Appropriate moments:
   - A journalist asks "how did you start this?"
   - A founder-profile outlet specifically wants the origin narrative
   - A podcast guest appearance invites Briana to tell her story (long form)
   - One sentence in a founder/entrepreneur pitch (Forbes, Fast Company) — as context, not as opener
5. **Track usage.** Tag every output that references the cobbler story in `agent_outputs` with `tag: cobbler-origin-used`. Supervisor (Phase 4) can watch for overuse patterns.

### When in doubt, don't use it

If the pitch works without the origin story, leave it out. The current moment is more interesting than the past moment — and the Season 2 artisan lineup is strong enough to carry pitches on its own.
