## Voice

### Core voice principles

1. **Specific beats polished.** One real observation about the sponsor's product beats three sentences of generic praise.
2. **Story over stats upfront.** The hook is the Slow Renaissance / cobbler origin / SF-in-the-AI-era narrative. Metrics belong in the one-pager or deck.
3. **Soft ask, clear next step.** Default CTA = "I'd love to send over our one-pager." Never a call, not a meeting — except where the playbook explicitly allows it.
4. **Founder-voiced, not press-release voiced.** First person. Contractions. One em-dash is fine, three is too many.
5. **No hype.** No "revolutionary," no "changing the game," no "the #1 podcast for..." The show sells itself.

### Tonal signatures to preserve

- **"I'm Briana Ottoboni, host of The Trades Show"** or **"I'm Briana, founder and host of The Trades Show"** — signature opener. The full first-and-last-name version lands better in cold outreach; the first-name-only version works for warm threads.
- **"San Francisco, the global epicenter of AI"** — Season 2 framing. Use when it's relevant to the sponsor.
- **"The handmade market is a $345B economy growing 8% a year"** — the one stat allowed in a cold email. One stat, not three.
- **"Warmly, Briana"** or **"Best, Briana"** — both are fine. Avoid "Cheers" or "Thanks!" sign-offs.
- **No exclamation marks in the body.** One in the subject line is the max, and even that is rare.
- **One em-dash per paragraph, max.** More reads affected.

### What every email does

- Uses the recipient's first name, spelled correctly
- References one specific, verifiable thing about their brand (see playbook § Personalization Minimums)
- Ties the sponsor to *why now, why us* — not generic "great fit"
- Closes with the right CTA (see § CTA Strategy below)
- Keeps body to 110–160 words for cold outreach; follow-ups can be shorter

### What every email avoids

- Inflated metrics or claims (no "over 2,000 and growing fast" — it's 2K, say 2K)
- Fabricated mutual connections or invented "I was just listening" moments
- Fake scarcity ("only 2 slots left") or fake deadlines
- Comparisons to competitors
- Flattery padding ("I love what you're building")
- Multiple links (one link max in body, usually thetradesshowpod.com or a specific episode)
- Calls as the cold-email CTA (with playbook exceptions)

## Format

### Length

- **Cold outreach (Touch 1):** 110–160 words body. Over 200 = too much. Under 100 = too thin.
- **Follow-up (Touch 2):** 60–100 words. Shorter.
- **Final check-in (Touch 3):** 40–80 words. Shortest.
- **Deck-send reply:** 60–100 words, one link, two to three calendar options if offering a call.
- **Close detail email:** 150–250 words, structured bullets.

### Structure of a cold pitch

```
[Subject line — specific to the sponsor, not generic]

Hi [First Name],

[Opening: specific verified observation about the sponsor — 1–2 sentences]

[Who you are: "I'm Briana, founder and host of The Trades Show…" — 1–2 sentences]

[Why them, why now: the specific alignment between their brand and the show — 1–2 sentences]

[Optional: one stat, if it fits naturally — audience overlap, market size, or a specific integration idea]

[CTA: one-pager send, deck send, or call offer per playbook rules]

Warmly,
Briana
```

### Subject line patterns

Good subject lines reference the sponsor specifically. Bad ones are generic.

**Patterns that work:**
- `A San Francisco love letter to craft — and a question for [Brand]`
- `[Brand] × The Trades Show — the artisans you'd meet`
- `Craft, the Analog Renaissance, and [Brand]`
- `[Mutual name] suggested we connect — The Trades Show × [Brand]`
- `A craft-forward podcast partnership for [Brand]`
- `Thought of [Brand] during our bladesmith episode` (follow-up #2 variant)

**Patterns to avoid:**
- `Sponsorship opportunity`
- `Partnership request`
- `Quick question`
- `Partnering with The Trades Show`
- Anything without the sponsor's name in it

### CTA strategy (the reconciled rule)

The default CTA is **"I'd love to send over our one-pager."** A one-pager is lower friction than a full deck, respects the reader's time, and is easier to forward internally.

**When to use each CTA:**

| Context | CTA |
|---|---|
| **Default cold outreach** | "I'd love to send over our one-pager" |
| **Warm intro (referral)** | "Happy to send our one-pager, and to hop on a quick 15 if it resonates" |
| **Large / enterprise brand where a one-pager is insufficient** | Offer both: "Happy to send our one-pager, or jump on a 20-minute call" |
| **Touch 2 (follow-up)** | Same as first, OR softer: "Happy to drop this thread if it's not a priority right now" |
| **Touch 3 (final check-in)** | No new ask — "Circle back next season?" or "Let me know if timing shifts" |
| **Deck-send reply (after interest)** | Send deck + offer 15-min call + 2–3 calendar options |
| **Post-call close detail** | Bulleted summary + agreement timeline |

**Never:**
- "Let me know if this is of interest" — too passive, no momentum
- "Let's jump on a call" as the cold CTA — too much commitment
- "Worth a 20-minute call?" as the default cold CTA — reserved for warm intros or enterprise

## Exemplars

### Exemplar A — Default cold outreach (SF local / mission-aligned)

```
Subject: A San Francisco love letter to craft — and a question for [Brand]

Hi [First name],

I've been a quiet admirer of [Brand] since [specific observation — a product, a store visit, a recent IG post]. The way you [specific thing they do well] is exactly the kind of intentionality my audience gravitates toward.

I'm Briana, founder and host of The Trades Show — a documentary series following San Francisco's artisans in their workshops during the AI boom. Season 2 is out now, with episodes featuring chefs, bladesmiths, glassblowers, surfboard makers, and bakers.

Our audience (81% women, 91% U.S., design-literate 30–45) is your customer. I'm reaching out because I'd love to explore a partnership for Season 2, and [Brand] feels genuinely aligned.

Would you be open to me sending over our one-pager? Happy to tailor it to [Brand] specifically.

Warmly,
Briana
```

**What to study:** specific observation opens; signature intro clause; audience demo stated cleanly; alignment stated; one-pager CTA; "Warmly, Briana" close.

### Exemplar B — Cold outreach with product alignment

```
Subject: [Brand] × The Trades Show — the artisans you'd meet

Hi [First name],

Every artisan we feature on The Trades Show runs a business, which means most of them use [Brand product category] in some form — and a few of our Season 2 guests specifically use [Brand]. That alignment is the reason I'm writing.

I'm Briana, the host and creator of The Trades Show, a docuseries on location with modern artisans in their workshops. We're in the middle of Season 2, set entirely in San Francisco — the global epicenter of AI — and the tension between handcraft and automation is the through-line.

Our audience is the exact demo [Brand] serves: small business owners, makers, design-forward professionals. I'd love to send over our one-pager and explore what an episode sponsorship could look like.

Open to that?

Best,
Briana
```

**What to study:** leads with the product-use alignment rather than narrative; shorter hook; same CTA; "Best, Briana" close (alternative sign-off).

Warmly,
Briana

**What to study:** the one approved stat (`$345B, 8% growth`) lands naturally without stat-stacking; heritage-brand framing; "would land beautifully" — confident without hype.

### Exemplar E — Touch 2 follow-up (episode-tied)

```
Subject: Thought of [Brand] during our bladesmith episode

Hi [First name],

I was editing our episode with bladesmith Elias Sideris this week — he talked about why mass-market knives are eroding the craft of cutlery, and why customers who find him tend to be the same people who seek out [Brand's category] with the same obsessive care.

It's the kind of episode where [Brand] wouldn't be interrupting the conversation — it would be part of it. The host-read slot is still open for that episode if the alignment lands.

Either way, thought you'd appreciate the framing. Happy to share the episode when it drops.

Briana
```

**What to study:** this is the model follow-up — content-driven, specific, connects to a real episode happening now, doesn't repeat the ask generically, naturally mentions an available slot without fake scarcity. Replaces the generic "bumping this up" approach.

### Exemplar F — Touch 2 warm follow-up (generic, if no episode tie)

```
Subject: Re: A San Francisco love letter to craft — and a question for [Brand]

Hi [First name],

Bumping this up in case it got buried — totally understand if sponsorship isn't the right fit or timing.

Quick update since my last note: [insert one piece of new context — new episode dropped, press mention, milestone, new guest lined up]. Felt worth sharing given the alignment with [Brand].

Happy to send the one-pager whenever works, or to drop this thread entirely if it's not a priority. No pressure either way.

Warmly,
Briana
```

**What to study:** replies on the same thread (subject starts with `Re:`); gives them a graceful exit ("drop this thread entirely"); adds real new context (not a generic bump); signals low pressure without desperation.

```
Subject: Re: [original thread]

Thanks so much for the interest — attached is our partnerships deck for Season 2. It lays out audience, guest lineup, tier structure, and a few notes on how sponsors show up in the show.

Happy to walk through it together if helpful. Here are a few 15-minute windows that could work:

Briana
```

**What to study:** sent within 24 hours of their reply; brief and warm; offers the call *as an option* paired with "async is easier" — respects their preference; three calendar options; "Looking forward to it" closes with energy.

### Exemplar I — Close detail email (post-call)

```
Hi [First name],

Really enjoyed our conversation on [day]. As discussed, here's where we landed:

- **Package:** Episode Partner for the [Guest Name] episode
- **Investment:** $1,500
- **Deliverables:** Host-read mid-roll (60–90 seconds), "brought to you by" intro credit, logo in credits, dedicated links in episode description, dedicated newsletter ad block, social post on launch day
- **Category exclusivity:** [Agreed category] for that episode
- **Launch date:** [Date]
- **Ad copy due:** [Date — 5 business days before launch]

I'll send a simple one-page agreement over tomorrow. Once that's signed, I'll share a short creative brief so we can write the ad together.

Excited to have [Brand] on for this one. The episode is going to be a good home for you.

Briana
```

**What to study:** structured bullet format for clarity; confirms every agreed term explicitly; sets next-step expectations; closes with genuine energy ("a good home for you"). This email protects both parties and becomes the reference when ad copy is due.

## Anti-exemplars — what NOT to do

- **Don't open with a compliment.** "I love your brand" is not personalization, it's noise. Lead with a specific observation instead.
- **Don't stat-stack.** One stat per cold email is the max. Three stats reads as a pitch deck dumped into email.
- **Don't ask for a call on the cold email** (unless it's a warm intro or enterprise exception — see playbook).
- **Don't use "per our last conversation"** on a cold open. It's a manipulation tell.
- **Don't write "just checking in"** as the entire email. Add value or don't send.
- **Don't fabricate mutual connections, prior meetings, or "I was just listening to your podcast" moments.**
- **Don't use urgency or scarcity** ("only 2 slots left," "this rate expires Friday") unless it's literally true.
- **Don't promise deliverables outside the defined tiers.**

## Pre-send checklist

Every draft must clear all of these before queueing for Briana's approval:

- [ ] Subject line references the sponsor (not generic)
- [ ] First name verified and spelled correctly
- [ ] Role/title verified within the last 90 days
- [ ] One specific, true observation about the sponsor in the opener
- [ ] Signature intro clause present ("I'm Briana, founder and host of The Trades Show…" or similar)
- [ ] Alignment statement specific to this brand (not "great fit")
- [ ] Specific episode or artisan pairing suggested where relevant
- [ ] CTA matches the context (one-pager default; call offer only on warm intros / enterprise)
- [ ] Body is 110–160 words (cold outreach) or appropriate shorter length (follow-ups)
- [ ] One link max in body
- [ ] No inflated metrics (2K is 2K, not "over 2,000 and growing fast")
- [ ] No fake scarcity, deadlines, or manipulation tactics
- [ ] No em-dashes over three per email; no exclamation marks in body
- [ ] "Warmly, Briana" or "Best, Briana" close
- [ ] Would Briana send this as written, or would she edit it?

If any box is unchecked, the draft is not ready. Revise.
